#### Nov 1914
- November 5: arrive at Grytviken Whaling Station on South Georgia

#### Dec
 - December 5 0845: weigh anchor and proceed from Cumberland Bay
- December 11: orient to Vahsel Bay, their destination 
- December 25: "Here endth another Christmas Day. I wonder how and undershot circumstances our next one will be spent. Temperature 30 degrees"

#### January 1915
- January 9: Pass a berg so magnificent they name it Rampart Berg (there are photos) so enormous the berg rose high above the sails of the ship
- January 15: within 200 miles of Vahsel Bay
- January 24: The Endurance is beset.  "As Orde-Lees, the storekeeper, put it, "frozen, like an almond in the middle of a chocolate bar."


#### Feb
- still fast; continuing to try to break up ice around ship and order watches so any sign of break up men can go to stations to work the ship free.
- February 24: Shackleton ends watches and decides the chances of getting free from the ice was not going to happen. They would be frozen there for the Antarctic Winter.

#### July
- July 21: the resulting force is called pressure - and it began on July 21.

#### August
- August 15: men still in high spirits
- August 31: The Endurance began to creak and groan like a haunted house.  But the Endurance had survived her second attack.  


#### September
- September 1: "She is stronger than we thought, and providing we don't get much heavier pressure...we should pull through all right"
- September 10: warmest day in 7 months - 1.9 degrees above zero.  And nearly 10 hours of daylight. 
- September 30: return of the pressure. The giant beams over head in the Ritz bent "like a piece of cane."

#### October
- October 14: "Endurance lay in small pool of open water - the first time since it was beset 9 months before."
- October 16: that the opening tendency of the pack justified getting up steam on the chance that they could force a way through. all hands were put to pumping up the boilers with water.
- October 18 dawned a misty, snowy morning. The lead ahead had disappeared, and the ice was a little closer. Throughout the day the ship felt little nips of pressure, but nothing serious. Then at 4:45 p.m., the floes on either side of the Endurance closed in against her, and kept on closing
   - In the space of five seconds, the Endurance was heeled over 20 degrees to port—and she continued to fall off. Worsley rushed to the lee rail and watched as plank after plank disappeared under the ice. Greenstreet stood nearby, ready to jump
- October 24, the pressure did arrive, it wasted no time. There had been pressure in the past, but nothing like this
   - About midnight, after twenty-eight hours of ceaseless work, McNeish finished his job, at least as well as it could be finished. But it only slowed the flow of water, and the pumps had to be kept going
- about October 26 a group of penguins walked up to the frozen ship and croaked a mournful sad cry at the ship and its crew.  None of these experienced Arctic explorers had ever heard such cries from penguins before.  It left them filled with an eerie feeling. 
	- The sailors stopped what they were doing, and old Tom McLeod turned to Macklin. "Do you hear that?" he asked. "We'll none of us get back to our homes again." Macklin noticed Shackleton bite his lip.
- October 27: "She's going, boys," he said. "I think it's time to get off." October 27 (see first highlight from chapter one)
	- "The date was October 27, 1915. The name of the ship wasEndurance. The position was 69° 5' South, 51°30' West — deep in the icy wasteland of the Antarctic's treacherous Weddell Sea, just about midway between the South Pole and the nearest known outpost of humanity, some 1,200 miles away."
